# Open Data

Open Data is OPI's standing program for making city data public. Through Open Baltimore, the City publishes datasets openly so residents, the Council, journalists, researchers, and partners can verify what the City reports and build on its data.

The program is not optional. [Baltimore City Code, Article 1, Subtitle 9](https://codes.baltimorecity.gov/us/md/cities/baltimore/code/1/9) establishes the Open Data Program and assigns it to the City's Chief Data Officer. OPI's Executive Director serves as Chief Data Officer, so the duties in the law sit with this office.

## What the law requires

Subtitle 9 sets a default of openness. Under § 9-6, the Chief Data Officer must run a program that proactively releases all publishable city data, free and in open formats, with no restrictions on how it is used or reused. "Open format" means nonproprietary, platform-independent, and machine-readable, so the data can be processed automatically rather than read one page at a time. The law also asks the program to publish with real documentation, to limit what is withheld while still protecting what must be protected, and to encourage people to build things with city data.

## What counts as publishable

The law sorts city data into three categories, and only one of them gets published.

| Category | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| Publishable | Neither protected nor sensitive. This is released proactively. |
| Protected | Data the City must withhold under the Maryland Public Information Act or other law. |
| Sensitive | Data whose release could raise privacy, confidentiality, or security concerns, or could jeopardize public health and safety, in a way that outweighs the benefit of publishing it. |

These categories are the legal test. OPI applies them through the four sensitivity tiers in the [Data Governance Framework](../../../about-us/our-teams/data-and-analytics/data-governance-framework.md), which is where a dataset actually gets classified. Classification is not permanent: data that cannot be released as collected is often publishable once it is aggregated, redacted, or de-identified.

## What agencies have to do

Subtitle 9 puts obligations on every City agency, not just on OPI. Under § 9-7, each agency:

- names a lead open data coordinator;
- keeps a current inventory of the datasets it holds and expects to publish;
- assesses the risk and the public benefit before treating information as sensitive;
- prioritizes which datasets to publish, weighing public interest, City priorities, the value of the data, and the cost of releasing it;
- reviews each dataset before publication for format, quality, timeliness, and the exclusion of protected and sensitive information;
- supports a public timeline for upcoming releases; and
- makes published datasets available for bulk download.

The Chief Data Officer oversees implementation and is required to create regular opportunities for agencies and the public to give feedback and collaborate.

## The portal

Open Baltimore is the City's official open data portal under § 9-8. Everything on it carries an open license: anyone may copy, publish, redistribute, modify, or use the data for any purpose, commercial or not. There is no permission to request and no use case to justify.

## The annual report

Under § 9-9, the Chief Data Officer reports to the Mayor and the City Council on or before June 30 each year. The report covers progress toward the program's goals, how well agencies are complying, the datasets currently published, and what is expected to publish in the year ahead, with a timeline. It is the program's main accountability moment, and it is public.

## What OPI does day to day

OPI publishes and maintains the datasets on Open Baltimore and sets the standards that keep them usable: plain language, accessibility, metadata, and a stated refresh schedule. It applies the classification that decides what can be released, generalizing or withholding precise or sensitive fields. It coordinates the agency open data coordinators and the inventory behind the annual report. And it feeds the [Citywide Data Network](../citywide-data-network/index.md), so partners and residents have something to build on.

## Limits worth knowing

The law is candid about what open data is and is not. Data on the portal is published for information only. The City makes no warranty about a dataset's completeness, accuracy, or fitness for any particular use, and is not liable for a dataset or for a third-party application built on one. Subtitle 9 also creates no private right of action, and failing to comply with it creates no liability for the City. These disclaimers are required to appear on the portal itself.

None of that lowers the bar OPI holds internally. The legal floor is a disclaimer. The working standard is data a resident or a Council member can rely on.
