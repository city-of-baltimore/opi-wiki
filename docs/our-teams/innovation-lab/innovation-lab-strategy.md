**EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

**Innovation as delivery, not theater**

The Innovation Lab is OPI’s citywide service improvement capability. It exists to identify service delivery problems, design and test practical solutions, and implement improvements that make government work better for residents and staff.

Innovation in city government has too often meant novelty, technology for its own sake, or special projects that drift without owners. Baltimore needs a different standard. Innovation should be structured problem-solving in service of delivery: discovery, design, prototyping, piloting, sustainment, and learning.

This strategy defines the Lab’s purpose, posture, methods, governance, and FY27 priorities. It is anchored in four commitments: start with the service, not the screen; design with users, not for them; build only when build is the right answer; and treat sustainment as the test of success.

**THE CASE FOR CHANGE**

**Many service problems are design problems**

Many of the problems residents feel every day are not caused by a lack of commitment. They are caused by systems that were not designed around the people using them. A resident may not know which agency owns a request. Staff may be working across systems that do not speak to each other. A supervisor may not have the data needed to manage a backlog. A process may have been built over years through workarounds instead of intentional design.

These are design problems, data problems, workflow problems, and ownership problems. They require more than a meeting. They require a method.

> *Performance tells us where the system is falling short. Data helps us understand why. The Innovation Lab designs and tests practical solutions. Cross-Agency Delivery helps implementation stick.*

**NORTH STAR**

**Practical innovation in service of delivery**

The Innovation Lab will be a disciplined, humane, learning-oriented practice that turns surfaced problems into tested improvements that residents and staff can see and feel.

Disciplined because every engagement starts with a real problem, a real user, a real owner, a real decision, and a plan for sustainment. Humane because we work alongside frontline staff and residents, not over them. Learning-oriented because we run small, evaluate honestly, and stop or scale based on evidence.

**GUIDING PRINCIPLES**

**How the Lab operates**

**1. Start with the service, not the screen.**

A digital product should not begin with a screen. It begins with a problem: who is trying to do what, where the service breaks, why it matters, and what would have to change for residents or staff to be better off.

**2. Design with users, not for them.**

We talk to residents and staff. We map services end to end. We test with the people who actually use them. A beautiful prototype that users cannot understand is not ready.

**3. Build only when build is the right answer.**

Not every problem should become a product. Sometimes the right answer is training, policy, procurement, staffing, a process change, or a better management routine. A good Lab is honest enough to say that.

**4. Run small, evaluate honestly.**

We prototype. We pilot. We listen again. We refine. We use Tiger Teams, design sprints, pilots, and data products to prove whether an improvement works in real conditions before we scale it.

**5. Sustainment is success.**

Launch is not success. Adoption and sustainment are success. A better workflow only counts if staff can keep using it. A new tool only counts if it is maintained. A process map only counts if it changes how people actually work.

**6. Reuse over rebuild.**

Components, patterns, APIs, design systems, and methods should be reused across products. Each engagement should leave the City with a stronger foundation for the next one.

**7. Honor public trust.**

The work happens in service of residents and staff. We do not build for spectacle. We build for delivery, and we explain the work plainly to the people it serves.

**WHAT THE LAB OWNS**

**Core capabilities**

**1. Service design and discovery.**

Field research, frontline listening, service blueprints, journey maps, root-cause analysis, and problem framing.

**2. Product management and engineering.**

Product briefs, UX flows, UI design, prototypes, engineering builds, QA, accessibility, security review, launch, and sustainment planning.

**3. Civic technology and AI pilots.**

Targeted pilots that use technology and AI to support staff and residents, governed by the City’s responsible AI review process.

**4. Tiger Teams and design sprints.**

Time-boxed engagements that bring together agencies, residents, designers, engineers, and analysts to diagnose, prototype, and pilot improvements.

**5. Strategic partnerships.**

Coordination with universities, civic technologists, philanthropic partners, and peer cities where partnerships expand capacity, accelerate learning, or unlock resources.

**6. Reusable patterns and design system.**

A growing library of components, accessibility patterns, intake flows, status communications, and operational tooling that future products can build on.

**WHAT THE LAB DOES NOT OWN**

**Boundaries that protect the work**

- It does not own performance accountability or CitiStat routines. Performance and CitiStat owns the executive rhythm.

- It does not own enterprise infrastructure, security, networking, or production environments. BCIT owns those.

- It does not own agency operations. Agencies own service delivery, staffing, and sustained workflow adoption.

- It does not own data governance, KPI definitions, or open data. Citywide Data & Analytics owns those.

- It does not own citywide narrative, Council coordination, or publication readiness. AdminOps owns those.

- It is not a place where unfunded special projects go to drift. Every engagement needs an owner, a decision, and a sustainment plan.

**OPERATING POSTURE**

**Discovery before build, sustainment from the start**

Every Lab engagement should have a clear admission test: a real problem, named users, a sponsor, a decision context, data needs, and a sustainment owner. We move from intake to discovery to service design to prototype to pilot to sustainment, and we plan the handoff at the start, not at the end.

> **Five Lab intake questions: What problem are we solving? Who experiences it? What decision or workflow will change? What does success look like? Who will own the product after launch?**

**HOW THE LAB PARTNERS ACROSS OPI**

**Integration with other services**

**With Performance and CitiStat**

The Lab takes problems surfaced in CitiStat and converts them into discovery, design, and pilot work. It returns evidence about what worked back into the performance routine.

**With Citywide Data & Analytics**

Data & Analytics provides shared datasets, methodology cards, KPIs, and AI/data readiness review. The Lab does not maintain enterprise data infrastructure; it consumes it through clear contracts.

**With Cross-Agency Delivery**

When a Lab engagement requires sustained sequencing across multiple agencies, Cross-Agency Delivery activates and runs implementation while the Lab continues to support design, evaluation, and product changes.

**With AdminOps**

AdminOps owns intake, portfolio visibility, public narrative, Council readiness, and publication. The Lab works through these channels rather than around them.

**CIVIC DESIGN AS LEADERSHIP**

**Why design and management belong together**

Civic design is not a decorative phase at the end of a project. It is a way of working. Leadership, at its best, is design: designing systems that are clear and accountable, teams that know how to work together, routines that create follow-through, and processes that do not break under pressure.

The Lab’s posture inside OPI is to make that mindset operational. We ask: What problem are we really solving? Who is this working for, and who is it not working for? Where are we adding friction? How do we redesign this so it works every time, not just when one heroic person pushes it through?

**RESPONSIBLE AI IN THE LAB**

**Pilots with guardrails**

AI is one tool among many. The Lab uses AI when it can support staff, reduce repetitive work, draft clearer resident communications, or surface patterns that help leaders see complexity more clearly. It does not use AI as theater or as a substitute for judgment.

Every Lab AI pilot follows the City’s responsible AI review process: problem framing, users, data sources, risk tier, decision impact, human oversight, evaluation method, logging, and sunset criteria. No production AI use case ships without a documented evaluation plan and a clear statement of where human judgment remains responsible.

**SUCCESS MEASURES**

**How we know the Lab is working**

- Engagement quality: share of engagements with a real problem, named user, owner, decision, sustainment plan, and admission test passed at intake.

- Delivery: prototypes, pilots, and products that move from discovery to launch with documented user testing and accessibility review.

- Service impact: measurable changes in cycle time, backlog age, repeat contacts, task success, or resident communication for priority services.

- Sustainment: share of products with named owners, runbooks, support models, data refresh plans, and review cadences after launch.

- Reuse: components, patterns, APIs, and methods reused across multiple products.

- Responsible AI: pilots reviewed with documented evaluation, human oversight, and sunset criteria.

- Learning: documented after-action reviews and decisions to scale, redesign, pause, or stop.

**FY27 PRIORITIES**

**What the Lab will do this year**

**1. Discovery and intake.**

Stand up disciplined intake, admission criteria, and a portfolio view that prevents drift and protects sustainment.

**2. Priority service redesigns.**

Lead service design and product work for 311 follow-up, permitting communication, and one cross-agency priority surfaced in CitiStat.

**3. Design system, version 1.**

Build a reusable design system with accessibility patterns, intake flows, status messaging, and components that future products can extend.

**4. Responsible AI pilots.**

Run a small number of governed AI pilots that improve real decisions or services, each with documented evaluation and human oversight.

**5. Tiger Team activations.**

Activate Tiger Teams for two priority cross-agency problems, with named authorizers, weekly sequencing, and sustainment plans.

**6. Civic partnerships.**

Establish a small set of strategic partnerships with universities, civic tech organizations, and philanthropic partners that expand capacity without creating shadow systems.

**7. Sustainment routine.**

Build a quarterly product review that closes, redesigns, or hands off products that are not delivering value.

**CLOSING**

**Innovation as a discipline**

The deeper promise of innovation is not the tool. It is the learning culture behind the tool. A learning government can admit when a service is not working, study the problem without blame, test a better approach, and change how it operates. A learning government does not confuse activity with impact. It asks whether residents and staff are actually better off because of the work.

> *Better government is not a one-time project. It is a discipline. The Innovation Lab is one of the ways we practice that discipline. Not as theater, but as delivery.*
