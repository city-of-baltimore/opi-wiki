**■ What CitiStat is — and is not**

CitiStat is Baltimore’s citywide performance management system: a disciplined, recurring conversation between an agency and the City Administrator’s leadership team, anchored on data, focused on a small number of priority outcomes, and ending with named follow-ups that get checked the next time. CitiStat is established in Baltimore City Code, Article 1, Subtitle 61.

> *Distributed operation. Single definition. Multiple OPI leaders may operate selected Stats where the topic aligns with their portfolio — but every Stat follows one common method, stewarded by the Performance team and authorized by the relevant Deputy Mayor.*

**Five things every Stat must have**

**A performance problem.** Not a status update. There is something we want to be different in the world, and the meeting is built around moving that thing.

**A decision-maker in the room.** The person empowered to decide, allocate, or unblock is present. If they cannot attend, the Stat is rescheduled.

**Reliable data.** Numbers we have agreed on, sourced from a system of record, current as of an agreed cutoff, and reviewed before the meeting.

**Pre-read discipline.** A pre-memo and the data pack go out at least 48 hours before the meeting. Participants come prepared.

**Closure mechanism.** Every commitment is logged with a name, a date, and a definition of done. The next Stat starts with last Stat’s commitments.

**What CitiStat is not**

- Not a status meeting. Status updates belong in agency operations, not in Stat.

- Not a dashboard review. The data is the prompt, not the agenda.

- Not a gotcha session. The tone is problem-solving. Discomfort comes from the problem, not from the room.

- Not a substitute for agency management. Agency directors own agency performance; OPI operates the routine.

**■ The Stat rhythm**

Every Stat runs on a steady cadence. Most Stats meet every four to six weeks. The cadence is the point: it is the regularity that produces accountability, not the intensity of any one meeting.

**Cadence at a glance**

| **Phase**                   | **Days from meeting** | **Owner**                  | **Output**                                               |
|-----------------------------|-----------------------|----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| Topic intake / scoping      | T‒30 to T−21          | Stat lead + Method steward | One-paragraph problem statement; metric set agreed.      |
| Data refresh + analysis     | T−20 to T−8           | Lead analyst               | Updated data pack; anomalies flagged.                    |
| Pre-memo drafted            | T−8 to T−4            | Stat lead + Lead analyst   | Draft memo; questions for the room finalized.            |
| Pre-read distributed        | T−4 to T−2            | Stat lead                  | Memo + data pack to agency and authorizing sponsor.      |
| Pre-brief                   | T−2 to T−1            | Stat lead with agency lead | Agency knows what will be asked; surprises removed.      |
| Stat meeting                | T0                    | Stat lead facilitates      | Decisions, commitments, and next-cycle metrics agreed.   |
| Read-out and commitment log | T+1 to T+3            | Lead analyst               | Commitments logged in CitiStat tracker; sponsor briefed. |
| Mid-cycle check             | T+10 to T+14          | Stat lead                  | Progress check on commitments; risks surfaced.           |
| Closure or carry-forward    | T+25 to T+30          | Stat lead + Method steward | Each commitment marked closed, carried, or escalated.    |

**■ The pre-memo**

The pre-memo is the Stat’s most important artifact. It is two to four pages. It tells the reader what we are looking at, what the data says, what we think is going on, what decision we are seeking, and what we will discuss in the room.

**Required sections**

**Problem statement.** One paragraph. What is the performance problem, in resident or service terms.

**Metrics in view.** The two to five metrics that anchor the conversation, with definition, source, and current value.

**What the data says.** A short narrative — trend, comparison, anomaly, hypothesis — with charts that earn their place.

**What we think is going on.** The leading explanations, with supporting evidence and counter-evidence.

**Open commitments.** Status of every commitment carried in from prior Stats.

**Decisions sought.** What the room is being asked to decide today, and who decides.

**Discussion questions.** Three to five sharp questions the facilitator will press on.

**Quality bar**

- Every chart and number has a source, an as-of date, and a definition.

- No more than five metrics in primary view. Supplementary metrics live in an appendix.

- Plain language. No jargon. Acronyms expanded on first use.

- Counter-evidence shown. If the leading hypothesis has a weakness, it is named.

**■ In the room**

A Stat lasts 60–90 minutes. The facilitator drives, the agency lead defends and decides, the authorizing sponsor weighs in on tradeoffs, and the analyst keeps the data honest. Everyone else is in the room because their voice is needed; observers are limited.

**Standard agenda**

**Frame.** The Stat lead names the problem, the question, and the desired outcome of the meeting (5 minutes).

**Data review.** The lead analyst walks the metric set and the key chart; agency confirms or contests (15 minutes).

**Diagnosis.** The room presses on cause — process, capacity, technology, policy, demand. Hypotheses get tested (20 minutes).

**Decisions and commitments.** The Stat lead names each commitment aloud, with owner and date, and confirms agreement (15 minutes).

**Carry-forward.** The lead analyst reads back the commitments. Open items from prior Stats are reconciled (5 minutes).

**Facilitation norms**

- Press on the problem, not the person.

- No commitment without a name and a date.

- If the data is contested, the room agrees on how the dispute will be resolved before next Stat — not in the room.

- If the room cannot decide, the Stat lead names the escalation path and moves on.

**■ After the meeting**

**Read-out**

Within three business days, the Stat lead sends a read-out to participants and the authorizing sponsor: decisions taken, commitments logged, open questions, and the date of the next Stat. The read-out goes into the CitiStat tracker and the agency’s record.

**Commitment tracking**

- Every commitment has an owner, a date, and a definition of done.

- Mid-cycle checks happen at the halfway point. Risks are surfaced early, not at the next Stat.

- Closures are evidenced. “Done” requires a link to the artifact, the data point, or the changed process — not a verbal report.

- Carry-forwards are explicit. If a commitment slips, the next Stat opens with why and what changed.

**Inspections and ground truth**

Where a Stat depends on field reality — trash collection, sweeping, code enforcement, traffic operations — the CitiStat Inspector conducts ride-alongs and site visits between meetings. Ground truth is the antidote to dashboard distortion: when the numbers and the street disagree, we go look.

**■ Stat portfolio standards**

OPI maintains a register of active and authorized Stats. Each Stat has a charter naming the problem, the participants, the cadence, and the metric set. The register is reviewed quarterly. New Stats are authorized by the Executive Director with the relevant Deputy Mayor.

**■ Common failure modes — and how to avoid them**

**Status creep.** The Stat starts to feel like a status meeting. Fix: name the performance problem at the top of every meeting; cut the standing tour of every metric.

**Performative accountability.** Tough talk in the room, no follow-up after. Fix: read commitments aloud; close the loop in writing within three days; track to evidence of done.

**Data theatre.** Charts that look impressive but do not move the conversation. Fix: every chart has to answer a specific question; cut the rest.

**Dilution of brand.** Every recurring meeting starts calling itself a Stat. Fix: hold the line on the five things; name other things working group, delivery room, product council.

**Leader-shaped problems.** Each Stat lead frames the problem in the shape of their discipline. Fix: the method forces the team to ask, what kind of problem is this, before deciding the intervention.
