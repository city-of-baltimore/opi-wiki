## Why QA matters

OPI’s work is used by senior leaders, agency partners, and sometimes the public. Quality is not optional, and it is not a separate phase. It is part of the work. This document describes the QA bar that applies to everything OPI publishes, internally and externally.

> *No public artifact ships without QA. No surprise launches.*

### Principles

- Quality is built in. QA is part of delivery, not an afterthought.

- Evidence over assertion. Reviewers verify claims; authors document the verification.

- Tested before shown. Demos and previews include disclosed known issues.

- Accessibility is a baseline. We do not ship work that excludes residents.

- Privacy is a first-class concern. Sensitivity is handled before publication, not after.

## What QA covers

Every artifact passes four gates before it is shared with an audience outside the immediate working team.

| **Gate**                | **What it confirms**                                                                                                                                |
|-------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Accessibility           | The artifact can be used by people with disabilities. Color contrast, alt text, headings, keyboard navigation, plain language complement.           |
| Plain language          | The artifact reads clearly to a smart reader who is new to city government. Jargon is defined or removed; sentences are short; the ask is up front. |
| Privacy and sensitivity | The artifact does not expose PII or restricted information. Sensitivity is labeled. Approvals are in place.                                         |
| Launch readiness        | Stakeholders are informed before publication. Comms are coordinated. Rollback or correction plan is in place. No surprise launches.                 |

## Gate 1 — Accessibility

### Minimum standard

- All public artifacts meet WCAG 2.1 AA contrast ratios (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text).

- All images include meaningful alt text. Decorative images are marked decorative.

- Documents and slides use proper heading hierarchy (H1, H2, H3) so screen readers can navigate.

- Tables have headers and a logical reading order.

- Web and digital experiences support keyboard navigation and focus indicators.

- PDFs are tagged. Scanned images of documents are not acceptable.

### Quick checks

- Read the artifact with a screen reader for one minute.

- Switch the document to grayscale. Is the meaning still clear?

- Tab through interactive content. Does focus move logically?

- Verify alt text is descriptive, not “image” or “photo.”

## Gate 2 — Plain language

### Minimum standard

- The ask, decision, or main finding appears in the first 50 words.

- Sentences average 20 words or fewer. Short sentences win.

- Jargon and acronyms are defined on first use, or replaced with plain words.

- Bullets and headings make the document skimmable.

- Voice is active. Subject–verb–object beats passive constructions.

- Numbers and dates are formatted consistently and use thousands separators.

### Quick checks

- A new hire who has never worked at OPI can read the artifact and explain it back accurately.

- The first paragraph could stand alone as a summary.

- Every claim is either obvious or sourced.

## Gate 3 — Privacy and sensitivity

### Minimum standard

- Sensitivity is labeled: Public, Internal, Restricted (PII), or Confidential.

- Restricted and Confidential artifacts use approved storage with access controls.

- PII is not stored in chat, email attachments, or unprotected drives.

- Aggregations and visualizations protect against re-identification.

- External-facing artifacts have explicit approvals from data owners and (where relevant) Law and Communications.

- Datasets published to Open Baltimore have completed the Open Data publication checklist.

### When to consult the Deputy Chief Data Officer designee

- Any new dataset combination or linkage involving residents.

- Any artifact that names individuals (residents, employees, vendors).

- Any data product crossing agency boundaries for the first time.

- Any visualization with cell sizes below the agreed minimum (typically \<10 records).

## Gate 4 — Launch readiness

### Minimum standard

- Stakeholders are informed before publication. Internal notice precedes external launch by at least one business day for routine work, more for sensitive work.

- Communications coordination is in place. Messaging is aligned across OPI, the agency, and (where relevant) the Mayor’s Office.

- A correction or rollback plan exists. We know how we would fix or retract the artifact if needed.

- A monitoring plan exists for digital releases (errors, traffic, feedback).

- A public narrative status is recorded in the Project Directory: Not public / Eligible / Drafting / Published.

### What “no surprise launches” means

- No one in leadership learns about an OPI public artifact from outside the building.

- Agency partners are informed before residents.

- Sensitive findings have a pre-publication briefing path.

## How QA is performed

### Roles

- **Author:** Owns the artifact. Initiates QA. Resolves findings.

- **Reviewer:** Independent eyes. At least one peer review for any artifact above Internal sensitivity.

- **Approver:** Confirms QA was performed and acceptance criteria met.

- **Manager:** Confirms QA pass before publication.

### Process

1.  Author drafts the artifact and runs the QA checklist.

2.  Author requests peer review. Reviewer documents findings.

3.  Author resolves findings or accepts known issues with rationale.

4.  Approver signs off. QA pass is recorded in the Project Directory or decision log.

5.  Launch readiness items (notice, comms, rollback) are confirmed.

6.  Artifact is published.

### QA artifacts kept on file

- QA checklist (completed and dated).

- Reviewer notes and the resolution of each finding.

- Sensitivity label and approvals (where applicable).

- Launch notice trail (who was informed, when).

## QA checklist (copy / paste)

### Accessibility

- Contrast meets WCAG AA.

- Headings are properly nested.

- Images have meaningful alt text.

- Tables have headers and logical order.

- PDFs are tagged.

### Plain language

- Ask is in the first 50 words.

- Average sentence is 20 words or fewer.

- Jargon and acronyms are defined or replaced.

- Layout is skimmable (headings, bullets).

- Voice is active.

### Privacy and sensitivity

- Sensitivity label applied.

- Storage and access controls match the label.

- PII handled per policy.

- External approvals captured.

- Re-identification risk reviewed.

### Launch readiness

- Internal notice provided.

- Comms coordination confirmed.

- Rollback or correction plan exists.

- Monitoring plan in place (digital releases).

- Public narrative status updated in the Project Directory.

## When QA fails

When an artifact fails QA, the failure and the resolution are documented. Repeat failures are coached, not punished privately. Patterns of failure are escalated to leadership and addressed at the system level (template, training, tooling), not just the individual artifact.

## Owner and review

Owned by Director’s Office with privacy and sensitivity standards co-owned by the Deputy Chief Data Officer. Reviewed semi-annually or after any significant incident.
