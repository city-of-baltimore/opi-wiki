# About Performance

{{ page_header(summary="How Baltimore turns priorities into measures, measures into decisions, and decisions into service improvements residents can feel.", category="ABOUT · TEAM") }}

The Performance team runs Baltimore's citywide performance management: the work of helping leaders and agencies set clear priorities, measure what matters, review progress with discipline, and follow through until services improve. Its flagship program is [CitiStat](../../../what-we-do/programs/citistat/index.md), the city's performance program, which the team runs day to day.

The team owns the method, not the agencies' operations. Agencies deliver services; Performance brings the structure, data, facilitation, and follow-through that turn a performance problem into a decision and a decision into a result.

## What the team does

Day to day, the team runs the CitiStat program for the citywide portfolio of Stats: the sessions, the pre-memos, the follow-up memos, and the tracking that keeps follow-ups from slipping. It supports agency performance planning, helping agencies set goals, KPI trees, owners, and review schedules aligned to the Mayor's Action Plan. It maintains and improves the City's performance measures, KPI definitions, and performance plans, and keeps the City's public performance products current. And it tracks every follow-up to closure with evidence, routing issues that need an executive decision, delivery, data support, or service redesign to the team that can handle them.

## How the work fits together

Performance management only matters if it changes decisions. Three jobs stay distinct and reinforce each other. The Mayor's Action Plan sets citywide priority outcomes. Agency Performance Plans translate those into each agency's annual commitments and measures. CitiStat finds where a required result is not moving, concentrates attention on a few problems, and drives the tactics to move them.

If a problem is not important enough to sit in the Mayor's Action Plan or an Agency Performance Plan, it is not important enough for CitiStat. Every Stat holds to the [four tenets](../../../what-we-do/programs/citistat/method-playbook.md#the-four-tenets-of-stat): timely, accurate intelligence shared by all; rapid deployment of resources; effective tactics and strategies; and relentless follow-up.

### The lightest routine that works

Not every performance question needs a standing Stat. Some belong in a one-time decision memo, a single delivery review, or a focused data sprint. The team's job is to route each issue to the lightest routine that will actually change the outcome, and to reserve CitiStat for problems that need recurring leadership attention, reliable-enough data, and follow-up over multiple cycles.

## The quality bar

A Stat only earns leadership's time if the numbers hold up. Every Stat product passes an independent review by the **CitiStat Program Manager** before it is presented. Data sources are checked for reliability and audited for how the data is actually collected and entered. Every figure names its source and pull date, code is documented so the analysis is reproducible, and visuals meet the City's standards for clarity and accessibility.

## What the team owns

The team owns Baltimore's performance management method and the routines that run it. That means the [CitiStat](../../../what-we-do/programs/citistat/index.md) program and its Stat cycle: the sessions, the pre-memos, the follow-up memos, and the tracking that keeps commitments from slipping. It owns the KPI standards for the City's priority performance measures, the support that helps agencies build and maintain their performance plans, and the follow-up that carries every commitment to closure with evidence.

## What the team does not own

The team owns the method, not the agencies' operations. Agencies run their own services; Performance brings the structure, facilitation, and follow-through. It does not own data engineering or the pipelines behind the numbers, which belong to [Data and Analytics](../data-and-analytics/index.md). It does not own product builds or service redesign, which belong to the [Innovation Lab](../innovation-lab/index.md). And it does not own long-horizon cross-agency delivery, which belongs to [Cross-Agency Delivery](../../../what-we-do/services/cross-agency-delivery/index.md). Performance works closely with all of them, but it does not replace them.

## How the team partners across OPI

Performance often sets the rest of the office in motion. It leans on [Data and Analytics](../data-and-analytics/index.md) for the reliable measures, dashboards, and data-quality notes behind every Stat. When a Stat surfaces a problem that needs a redesigned service or a new tool, it hands that work to the [Innovation Lab](../innovation-lab/index.md). When a problem spans several agencies, it routes the work to [Cross-Agency Delivery](../../../what-we-do/services/cross-agency-delivery/index.md). And it relies on AdminOps for scheduling, public summaries, and the annual reporting that keeps the Stat portfolio visible.

## What success looks like

Success is a performance system that is useful, trusted, and acted on outside the meeting. Sessions happen as scheduled, pre-memos and follow-up memos arrive on time, and follow-ups are tracked and closed with evidence. Those sessions produce decisions, follow-ups, or measurable learning, not status recaps. Priority measures each carry an owner, a formula, a source, a review schedule, and their caveats. Priority services show gains residents can feel: shorter cycle times, smaller backlogs, fewer repeat problems. And leadership relies on the numbers, while residents and the Council can see what changed.
