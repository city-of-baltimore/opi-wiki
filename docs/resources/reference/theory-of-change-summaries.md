# Theory of Change Summaries

## Portfolio, service, program, and team language

A portfolio is how OPI organizes people, leadership, and cost centers. A service is what OPI delivers to City leadership, agencies, residents, and partners. A program is a formal named body of work, such as CitiStat or Open Data. A team is the staff group that performs the work.

Use this distinction when writing or editing OPI pages. It prevents Cross-Agency Delivery from being described like a staffed portfolio, keeps CitiStat from being treated as all of performance management, and keeps the Innovation Lab from becoming a catch-all for any special project.

**■ How to read this document**

A theory of change is a step-by-step explanation of how the work we do leads to the change we want to see. It names what we put in, what we do, what we produce, what improves as a result, and the long-term impact we are aiming at — and it surfaces the assumptions that have to hold for the chain to work. This document presents one summary per service. The fuller versions live in each service’s service definition.

**■ Service summaries**

**1. Citywide Performance Management**

**NORTH STAR**

> *Distributed operation. Single definition. Every Stat is a problem-solving conversation that ends with commitments and proof of closure.*

**The problem we exist to address**

Without a disciplined rhythm, agencies optimize what gets measured, not what matters. Service problems persist because no recurring forum forces decisions, names owners, and tracks follow-through.

**Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact**

**Inputs.** CitiStat analysts and inspectors; agency leads; Deputy Mayor sponsors; reliable agency data; the CitiStat method.

**Activities.** Run authorized Stats on a steady cadence; produce pre-memos; facilitate decisions; conduct inspections; track commitments to closure.

**Outputs.** Pre-memos and read-outs; commitment logs; inspection reports; agency performance plans; the CitiStat portfolio register.

**Outcomes.** Faster service delivery on prioritized lines; fewer recurring breakdowns; clearer agency goals; sustained accountability across leadership turnover.

**Impact.** Baltimore residents experience reliable, predictable services; the City Administrator has a real-time picture of where service delivery is improving and where it is not.

**Core assumptions**

- Decision-makers will attend regularly and act on commitments.

- Agency data is reliable enough — or can be made reliable through Stat — to anchor the conversation.

- A common method, faithfully applied, beats a more elaborate but inconsistently applied one.

- Inspections and ground truth are credible counterweights to dashboard distortion.

**How we will know it is working**

- Closure rate of commitments by the next Stat.

- % of primary stakeholders who say the Stat is valuable.

- Trend in the agency’s priority operational metrics.

- Agencies adopting the same metric and routine in their internal management.

**2. Citywide Data and Analytics**

**NORTH STAR**

> *Trusted data, in the hands of decision-makers, on the questions that matter.*

**The problem we exist to address**

City data is fragmented across systems, of uneven quality, and hard to use without specialist help. Decisions get made on stale numbers or none. Public trust in city numbers is fragile.

**Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact**

**Inputs.** Data engineers, scientists, and product managers; the City data platform and warehouse; agency systems of record; data governance partners.

**Activities.** Stand up the citywide data warehouse; build governance and a catalog; produce decision-grade analytics products; train agencies; publish open data.

**Outputs.** Data warehouse, data products and dashboards, governance policies, an open data portal, training curricula, internal tools (e.g., the late-payments dashboard, the ORF tracker, the permit-monitoring tool).

**Outcomes.** Decisions made on trusted, current numbers; faster cycle times in priority workflows; resident-facing transparency; reduced agency dependence on bespoke pulls.

**Impact.** Baltimore is a data-capable city: residents, agencies, and partners can see how the city is performing and contribute to its improvement.

**Core assumptions**

- Agencies will share data and adopt governance once value is demonstrated.

- Investment in platform and quality compounds; one-off analyses do not.

- Internal product management beats external contractors for sustained use.

- Open data and internal data are mutually reinforcing, not in tension.

**How we will know it is working**

- Number of agencies with priority datasets in the warehouse.

- Time to a decision-grade answer on a priority question.

- Adoption of OPI dashboards by agency leadership in their own routines.

- Reduction in duplicate, ad-hoc data pulls.

**3. Innovation Lab**

**NORTH STAR**

> *From service problems to practical solutions — designed with frontline staff and residents, prototyped fast, handed off to operate.*

**The problem we exist to address**

Service problems often look like technology, policy, or staffing problems in isolation; they are usually all three. Without a small team that can hold the whole problem and design across boundaries, agencies cannot move from analysis to action.

**Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact**

**Inputs.** Civic designers; product managers; engineers; access to frontline staff and residents; Public Innovation Toolkit; partnership with BCIT and agencies.

**Activities.** Run tiger teams on priority service problems; prototype solutions in real conditions; build internal digital products; map and redesign processes; train agencies in design and delivery methods.

**Outputs.** Process maps and reform plans; prototypes and pilots; in-house digital products (e.g., 311 reforms, parking enforcement workflow, traffic calming intake, Downtown RISE tools); operating-model handoffs to agencies.

**Outcomes.** Faster, clearer service experiences; fewer manual handoffs; agencies absorb improved processes and own them after handoff; a small portfolio of credible, visible wins per year.

**Impact.** A reputation for delivery: a city government known for shipping practical improvements where residents can see and feel them.

**Core assumptions**

- Six-month time-boxed projects beat indefinite ones.

- Frontline staff and residents are essential co-designers, not interview subjects.

- Agencies can absorb a finished product if the handoff is planned from the start.

- In-house build is feasible and preferable for a defined class of city problems.

**How we will know it is working**

- Number of tiger teams completed on schedule with a sustain plan in hand.

- Adoption of designed processes and tools by agencies after handoff.

- Cycle-time reductions in piloted workflows.

- Resident satisfaction on the targeted service experience.

**4. Cross-Agency Delivery**

**NORTH STAR**

> *When the problem crosses agency lines, OPI runs the delivery chain.*

**The problem we exist to address**

Many of the city’s hardest problems — permits, vacants, downtown recovery, 311 — do not sit inside one agency. Without a designated quarterback, the work falls into the seams between agencies and stalls.

**Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact**

**Inputs.** PMO and program managers; CitiStat method; design and analytics capacity; mayoral and City Administrator authority; partner agencies’ frontline teams.

**Activities.** Stand up cross-agency delivery reviews; sequence dependencies; integrate data, design, and performance routines; broker decisions; report to the City Administrator and the Mayor.

**Outputs.** Cross-agency delivery plans; integrated dashboards (e.g., Downtown RISE); reform packages (e.g., permit reform); coordinated execution calendars; closure memos.

**Outcomes.** Multi-agency initiatives ship on time; dependencies are surfaced and resolved early; agencies have a shared picture of progress and risk.

**Impact.** The city can pursue ambitious cross-agency outcomes — not just agency-by-agency improvement.

**Core assumptions**

- Cross-agency work needs a designated quarterback with executive backing.

- Decision rights, sequenced clearly upfront, prevent most of the friction.

- Performance, design, and data routines integrated together outperform any one alone.

- OPI’s authority is borrowed from the Mayor and CA; we must use it sparingly and visibly.

**How we will know it is working**

- On-time milestone delivery on cross-agency programs.

- Reduction in escalations to the City Administrator on coordination matters.

- Agency leaders reporting clarity on decision rights and sequencing.

- Visible progress on the small set of named cross-agency priorities.

**5. AdminOps**

**NORTH STAR**

> *OPI runs as well as the city we are asking other agencies to be.*

**The problem we exist to address**

A team that asks agencies to operate with discipline must hold itself to the same standard. Without strong intake, project management, communications, and people operations, OPI cannot deliver the other four services reliably.

**Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact**

**Inputs.** Chief of Staff; PMO; communications and partnerships; HR and finance partners; SharePoint and Microsoft 365 platform; the OPI Operating Model.

**Activities.** Operate the single front door for incoming work; run the project gate process; manage hiring, onboarding, offboarding, and people operations; steward SharePoint and the OPI Wiki; communicate with agency partners, City Council, and the public.

**Outputs.** Intake decisions and charters; the Project Directory; onboarding and offboarding flows; performance standards; comms artifacts; the Wiki and SharePoint structure; budget, hiring, and reporting deliverables.

**Outcomes.** Work enters cleanly and is sized appropriately; OPI staff onboard fast and stay productive; the team’s knowledge survives turnover; partners and the public have clear, consistent information about OPI.

**Impact.** A reliable internal operating system that lets the four citywide services produce their best work.

**Core assumptions**

- A single front door beats freelance project starts.

- Documented processes survive leadership transitions.

- Investing in OPI’s own ops returns time to the citywide services.

- Communications discipline (one voice, consistent language) compounds trust over time.

**How we will know it is working**

- Intake-to-charter cycle time.

- Onboarding ramp time for new hires.

- Project gate adherence across the OPI portfolio.

- Stakeholder reports of clarity on OPI’s role and how to engage.

**■ How we will test the theory**

Each service’s assumptions become things we look at in our quarterly review. Where an assumption is not holding, we adjust the activities, not just the outputs. The point of a theory of change is not to be right — it is to be honest about why we believe what we are doing will work, and to find out quickly when it does not.

- Quarterly: review signals against targets; revise activities where signals lag.

- Annually: revisit assumptions; rewrite where the world has changed.

- At leadership transition: re-confirm the theory of change with new sponsors before changing the activities.
