# Copy Review — CitiStat Program (`docs/what-we-do/programs/`)

Senior-editor review against a Bloomberg + HKS target: crisp, factual, active, concrete, plain. Recommendations only — no files edited. Each rec is tagged **[safe]** (wording fix, meaning preserved) or **[judgment]** (changes meaning/tone/structure — owner decides). The `**Term** — definition` em-dash pattern is accepted house style and is not flagged; only prose-flow em-dash overuse is.

Pages reviewed: 27 — `programs/index.md`, `citistat/index.md`, `strategic-framework.md`, `method-playbook.md`, `method-staff-quick-reference.md`, `quality-standards.md`, `templates.md`, `portfolio/index.md`, and the 19 per-Stat briefs.

## 3. `strategic-framework.md`

**Assessment.** This is the longest, most aspirational page and the one most at risk of over-writing — and it does drift there in places. The bones are strong (the From/To shift cards, the legal-mandate table, the operating-cycle table all land), but the prose sections lean on motivational cadence ("rigorous, humane, modern, and useful"), triplet/quadruplet adjective stacks, and a few near-verbatim repeats of the Method Playbook. Tighten the connective prose and the page sharpens considerably.

**Recommendations.**

- [judgment] "*The next phase of CitiStat is not about adding meetings. It is about building a disciplined, humane, learning-oriented performance system. When the routine works, agencies experience it as help, not heat. Leadership gets decision points, not status updates. Residents experience services that become faster, clearer, more reliable, and easier to trust.*" This is the most sermon-like paragraph on the page. The "agencies experience it as help, not heat / Leadership gets decision points, not status updates" antithesis is good and earns its place. But "faster, clearer, more reliable, and easier to trust" is a four-adjective stack. → trim to "Residents get services that are faster, clearer, and easier to trust."

- [safe] "*This document consolidates the CitiStat strategic argument for City leadership, Council, agency staff, partners, and public-facing use.*" — mixed list (audiences + "public-facing use" is not an audience). → "...for City leadership, Council, agency staff, partners, and the public."

- [judgment] "*It also absorbs the case for treating CitiStat as a learning and delivery loop, not a meeting series.*" "absorbs the case for" is abstract/AI-flavored. → "It also makes the case for treating CitiStat as a learning and delivery loop, not a meeting series." (And note: "learning and delivery loop" / "learning and accountability loop" / "performance operating system" are used somewhat interchangeably across the page — see consistency note.)

- [safe] Executive-summary section heading "Executive summary: a learning and delivery loop" then the very next major heading is "What CitiStat is: a learning and accountability loop." Two different "loop" labels in two adjacent headings reads like a near-miss. → align the noun (pick "learning and accountability loop" or "learning and delivery loop" and use it consistently).

- [safe] "*CitiStat addresses those failure modes by creating a predictable cadence for truth, decision-making, and follow-through.*" "a predictable cadence for truth" is a touch grand/abstract. → "...a predictable cadence for facts, decisions, and follow-through." (concrete nouns, parallel).

- [safe] "*Connect resident signals, frontline experience, and operational data so performance does not become paper performance.*" "paper performance" appears twice on the page and again in the Playbook; it's vivid but make sure it's defined once. Fine as-is here; flagging the repetition.

- [judgment] Section "Data discipline: from tracking to understanding" — "*The goal is not to count everything. The goal is to understand the signals that help leadership and agencies act.*" The "The goal is not X. The goal is Y." construction appears at least four times across this page and the Playbook ("The goal is not more meetings…" recurs verbatim). It's an effective rhetorical move once, but by the fourth instance it reads as a tic. → keep one or two; vary the rest.

- [safe] "*Slides are useful for framing a conversation. They are not the long-term memory of the performance system.*" Strong, keep. But the section then says "*CitiStat should increasingly rely on reusable data products, living notebooks, dashboards, methodology cards, process maps, and commitment trackers that can be searched and reused.*" — "searched and reused" after "reusable data products" is redundant. → "...process maps, and commitment trackers that can be searched and reused" → drop "reusable" earlier or "reused" later.

- [safe] "*A new analyst should be able to understand a Stat topic in 30 minutes*" — good, concrete, keep.

- [judgment] "A humane CitiStat" section: "*Frontline workload, training gaps, morale, informal workarounds, union considerations, staffing realities, and resident experience are not side issues. They are evidence.*" Seven-item list before the payoff. The payoff ("They are evidence.") is excellent. → trim the list to four or five of the strongest items so the punch lands faster.

- [safe] "*A humane CitiStat model asks hard questions without assuming bad intent.*" "model" is filler here. → "A humane CitiStat asks hard questions without assuming bad intent."

- [judgment] Closing section "Closing: a discipline for the next chapter": "*CitiStat is one of Baltimore's most important civic management traditions. Its next chapter should be rigorous, humane, modern, and useful. It should help the City learn faster, decide more clearly, act more consistently, and tell the public what changed.*" This is the page's most motivational-filler-prone passage: "rigorous, humane, modern, and useful" is a four-adjective stack, and "learn faster, decide more clearly, act more consistently" is a triplet of adverbs. The pull-quote that follows ("Measure what matters. Fix what is broken. Follow through until the improvement sticks.") already delivers the punch. → consider cutting the adjective stack: "Its next chapter should be rigorous, humane, and modern" or letting the pull-quote carry it and trimming the lead-in sentence.

- [safe] "*The strongest CitiStat portfolio is not the biggest portfolio.*" — good, keep. Section title "Topic selection: fewer, sharper, stickier" — "stickier" is colloquial; it works given the surrounding sustainment language, but in a Bloomberg/HKS register "fewer, sharper, more durable" reads cleaner. [judgment]

- [safe] "*the routine helps City leadership and agencies:*" then a bulleted list where several bullets start with strong verbs (Spot, Clarify, Build, Reduce, Connect, Document) — this list is well-built; no change.

- [judgment] The "How CitiStat connects to OPI" table and the "What CitiStat is" compare-cards both restate the is/is-not framing that the Playbook's operating-principles table also covers. Not wrong, but the Framework and Playbook are each ~independently restating "CitiStat is a routine that turns signals into decisions, commitments, follow-through" three-plus times each. Pick the canonical one-line definition (the pull-quote "Working definition" in the Playbook is the cleanest) and let the others reference it rather than re-paraphrase.

## 4. `method-playbook.md`

**Assessment.** The strongest-written long page in the set — operational, concrete, table-driven, low on filler. Voice hits the target. The main issues are (a) overlap with the Strategic Framework on the "what is CitiStat" definition and the four tenets, and (b) a few intro sentences that editorialize before the tables do the real work.

**Recommendations.**

- [safe] "*The playbook exists to make excellence repeatable.*" → this exact phrase is also the closing section heading ("Closing: make excellence repeatable") and the pull-quote. Used three times. Keep the closing instance (it's the payoff); soften the opener to avoid pre-spending the line, e.g. "This playbook makes a good Stat repeatable, so a session does not depend on personality, memory, or heroic follow-up."

- [safe] "*CitiStat should feel rigorous and humane.*" / section title "Operating principles: rigorous and humane." "feel" is slightly soft for the register. → "CitiStat is rigorous and humane." (active, declarative).

- [safe] "*The work must be grounded in facts, but it should never become a ritual of blame.*" — good, keep.

- [judgment] "*The best sessions create a high-accountability, high-support environment*" — "high-accountability, high-support environment" is the one piece of management-consulting jargon in an otherwise plain page. → "The best sessions hold agencies accountable while helping them clear the barriers they cannot resolve alone." (the sentence's own next clause already says this, so this is partly a de-dupe).

- [safe] Cabinet attendance: "*Standing roles invited across the cabinet include the Chief Human Capital Officer (DHR), Labor Commissioner (OLC), Chief Information Officer (BCIT)…*" — long but necessary (names/acronyms are load-bearing). No change; do not touch the acronyms.

- [safe] "Data standards and evidence": "*The goal is not perfect data before action; the goal is reliable-enough data with transparent limitations.*" — good, keep. (This is the cleanest instance of the "the goal is not X; the goal is Y" pattern.)

- [safe] "*Data on a screen is necessary but not sufficient.*" — good, keep.

- [safe] "Issue selection" margin note: "*Stats live or die by these tests.*" — strong, keep. Whole margin note is well-voiced.

- [judgment] The "four tenets" appear in full (with the same CompStat/1999 framing) in BOTH the Framework (§The four tenets of Stat) and the Playbook (§The four tenets of Stat, with the tenet cards). The Framework even says "The Method Playbook is the official reference." Good — but the Framework still restates all four tenet names inline. Consider trimming the Framework instance to a one-line pointer and letting the Playbook own the full definitions.

- [safe] "*Templates and quality bar*" section duplicates two sentences that also appear on `quality-standards.md` verbatim ("Every Stat product has a named lead and a named QCer; the QCer is not the same person who built the work. The lead and the QCer sign off before materials go out."). Intentional cross-reference is fine, but verbatim duplication across two pages risks drift — consider stating it once (Quality Standards) and pointing to it here.

## 9. The 19 per-Stat briefs (`portfolio/*.md`)

**Assessment.** Strong parallelism overall — this is the most disciplined section. Every brief uses the identical skeleton (metadata table → Public Purpose → Staff Purpose → Core Management Questions → Starter Focus Areas → Standard Artifacts → Tiger Team / Delivery Triggers), and the "Standard Artifacts and Data Products" block is correctly boilerplate across all 19. Length is consistent (all ~50 lines). The voice is terse and factual as intended. The drift that exists is small and mostly in the Public/Staff Purpose paragraphs.

### Section-level consistency findings

- **[judgment] Metadata label inconsistency — the most important portfolio finding.** On the per-Stat pages the field is labeled **"OPI Operating Lead"** but is populated with a *support-model* value ("Data Team-led," "Director's Office," "Performance Team"), while a *separate* **"OPI Support Model"** row holds the fuller string. Example, 311 Stat: `OPI Operating Lead | Data Team-led` and `OPI Support Model | OPI-operated / Data Team-led`. But the portfolio index's metadata definitions say **OPI Operating Lead** = "OPI portfolio or person responsible for running or coordinating the Stat" and the master register column is titled **"OPI Lead."** So three labels ("OPI Operating Lead," "OPI Lead," "OPI Support Model") and the values don't cleanly map. Recommend the owner reconcile: either rename the per-Stat row to "OPI Support Model / Lead" or split it so "Operating Lead" carries a role/portfolio and "Support Model" carries the operated-by string. Not a wording fix — flagging for the owner.

- **[safe] "Director's Office" vs "Director's Office-led."** Fire/EMS, Police, and Youth-adjacent pages render the operating-lead value as "Director's Office" (no "-led") while the master register column uses "Director's Office-led." Workplace Safety uses "Director's Office-led with Performance method support." Normalize the suffix.

- **[safe] Em-dash em / brand name "Fire & EMS" vs "Fire and EMS."** The page title and `page_header` use "Fire and EMS," but the body prose uses "Fire & EMS Stat" throughout. Same split on "HR & Labor" (title "HR and Labor," body "HR & Labor"). Pick one rendering of the ampersand for body prose vs. title and apply consistently. (Do not change the table values that may be load-bearing acronyms.)

- **[safe] Public Transparency Level phrasing drifts.** Values range from a single word + sentence ("High. Publish…") to "Moderate to high. Publish…" to "Low to moderate. Publish…" — that variation is legitimate (real differences in posture). But the sentence style drifts: some say "Publish public summaries…" (redundant "public… public"), e.g. Solid Waste, Housing, Youth, Rec. → drop the second "public": "Publish summaries, maps, and what-changed updates where appropriate."

- **[safe] "what changed updates" vs "what-changed updates."** 311, Clean, Solid Waste use the unhyphenated "what changed updates," which reads as a parsing stumble. → hyphenate "what-changed updates" everywhere for consistency.

## Cross-page themes (summary for the owner)

1. **Verbatim duplication across pages and within pages.** Several signature sentences are copy-pasted: the "every part of OPI" chain (programs/index ↔ citistat/index), the "CitiStat is managed by the Executive Director…" governance line (strategic-framework ↔ portfolio/index), the "named lead and named QCer" pair (method-playbook ↔ quality-standards), and "The goal is not more meetings…" (twice on portfolio/index, plus the Framework). Pick one canonical home for each and reference rather than repeat — this is the highest-leverage cleanup.

2. **The "The goal is not X; the goal is Y" / "is… is not" rhetorical pattern is overused.** Powerful once or twice (it's genuinely good in the Quick Reference and the Playbook's data-standards line); by the fourth+ instance across the Framework and portfolio it reads as a tic. Keep the best instances, vary the rest.

3. **Adjective/verb stacks and over-long noun lists.** Concentrated in the Strategic Framework's connective prose (four-adjective closings) and in the per-Stat "Staff use X Stat to [9–10 nouns]" sentences. Capping lists at ~five or six items and cutting trailing abstractions ("support accountable government operations," "fully functional," "rigorous, humane, modern, and useful") sharpens the register noticeably without losing content.

The corpus is already close to the target voice — the Quick Reference and the per-Stat metadata skeleton are exemplary. The work is trimming, de-duplicating, and reconciling labels, not rewriting.
